resetViewport
switchView "equations"
renderText "title" "The Central Limit Theorem" "#FFFF00" 0 -40
renderEquation "subtitle" "Order\ Out\ of\ Chaos" "#9A72AC" 0 40
say "The Central Limit Theorem is one of the most beautiful results in all of mathematics."
renderEquation "setup" "S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_n" "#58C4DD" 0 140
say "Imagine you have a random variable. It could be anything: rolling a die, picking a random number, flipping coins."
say "Now, what happens when you add many of these random variables together?"
transformEquation "setup" "S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_n \quad \longrightarrow \quad ?" 2000 "#58C4DD" 0 140
say "No matter what shape the original distribution has, the sum always converges to the exact same, elegant curve."
say "Let's watch this miracle unfold, starting from the most boring distribution imaginable."
wait 1500
switchView "desmos"
zoomToPoint 1/2 1/2 3
plotEquation "uniform" "0 \le x \le 1 \{0 \le y \le 1\}" "#58C4DD"
plotEquation "xax" "y=0" "#FFFFFF"
say "Here is a single uniform random variable. Every value between zero and one is equally likely."
say "Notice: it's completely flat. No peak, no bell shape. Nothing special at all."
animateDottedEquation "sweepline" "x=a" "a" 0 1 "#FFFF00" 3000
say "A vertical sweep confirms: the probability is the same everywhere. Perfectly boring."
free "sweepline"
wait 1000
say "Now, what happens when we add two of these flat random variables together?"
animateEquationMorph "uniform" "0 \le x \le 1 \{0 \le y \le 1\}" "0 \le x \le 2 \{0 \le y \le (1-\abs(x-1))\}" "h" "#58C4DD" 3000
zoomToPoint 1 1/2 4
say "The flat top vanishes! A triangle appears."
say "Getting an extreme sum, like zero or two, requires both variables to be at their extremes. That's rare."
say "But getting a sum near one can happen in many different ways. So the middle is favored."
wait 1500
say "Two variables made a triangle. Let's add a third."
animateEquationMorph "uniform" "0 \le x \le 2 \{0 \le y \le (1-\abs(x-1))\}" "0 \le x \le 3 \{0 \le y \le \max(0, (x^2/2)\{x \le 1\} + (-2x^2+6x-3)/2 \{1<x\}\{x \le 2\} + (3-x)^2/2 \{2<x\})\}" "h" "#83C167" 3000
zoomToPoint 3/2 1/2 5
say "The sharp point rounds off. The hard corners stretch into smooth tails. It's starting to look like a bell."
wait 1000
say "And with four variables, the edges smooth away almost completely."
animateEquationMorph "uniform" "0 \le x \le 3 \{0 \le y \le \max(0, (x^2/2)\{x \le 1\} + (-2x^2+6x-3)/2 \{1<x\}\{x \le 2\} + (3-x)^2/2 \{2<x\})\}" "0 \le x \le 4 \{0 \le y \le \max(0, x^3/6 \{x \le 1\} + (-3x^3+12x^2-12x+4)/6 \{1<x\}\{x \le 2\} + (3x^3-24x^2+60x-44)/6 \{2<x\}\{x \le 3\} + (4-x)^3/6 \{3<x\})\}" "h" "#83C167" 3000
zoomToPoint 2 1/3 6
say "With just four random variables summed, the shape is already remarkably smooth and bell-like."
say "Each addition acts as a smoothing operator, erasing the sharp geometry and pulling everything toward a universal curve."
wait 1500
free "uniform"
free "xax"
switchView "equations"
renderText "title2" "The Universal Attractor" "#FFFF00" 0 -40
say "As n grows, this shape approaches a very specific mathematical function."
renderEquation "gauss" "f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\, e^{-x^2/2}" "#58C4DD" 0 60
say "This is the Standard Normal Distribution, also called the Gaussian."
say "The exponential of negative x squared creates the perfect bell shape: tall in the middle, fading symmetrically to zero on both sides."
renderEquation "standardize" "Z_n = \frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}" "#83C167" 0 160
say "If we standardize our sums, shifting them to center at zero and scaling them so they don't just spread infinitely wide, they converge exactly to this curve."
renderEquation "limit" "Z_n \xrightarrow{\ d\ } \mathcal{N}(0,\,1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty" "#FC6255" 0 250
say "This is the Central Limit Theorem. Let's see how close we already are."
wait 1500
switchView "desmos"
zoomToPoint 0 0 8
plotEquation "gaussian" "y=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-x^2/2}" "#FFFF00"
say "Here is the perfect Gaussian bell curve."
setVariable "n" 1
plotEquation "approx" "y=\frac{\sqrt{n}}{\sqrt{1/12}} \cdot \max(0, (x\cdot\sqrt{n\cdot(1/12)}+n/2)^{(n-1)} \cdot \sum_{k=0}^{n} (-1)^k \cdot \binom(n,k) \cdot \max(0, x\cdot\sqrt{n/12}+n/2-k)^{(n-1)} / ((n-1)! \cdot n))" "#58C4DD"
say "Now watch. We begin with a single flat uniform variable, standardized to sit at the origin."
animateVariable "n" 1 30 8000
say "As n increases from one to thirty, watch the blue curve morph. It starts as a flat block, becomes a triangle, then smooths out and wraps tighter and tighter around the yellow Gaussian."
say "By around n equals ten, the two curves are nearly indistinguishable."
say "And as n approaches thirty, they become effectively identical."
stopVariableAnimation "n"
say "This is the Central Limit Theorem in action. No matter how we started, the sum always finds its way to the same beautiful bell curve. Order emerges from chaos."
